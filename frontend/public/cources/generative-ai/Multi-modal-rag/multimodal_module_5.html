01 — Goal and Core Problem

Why Storage and Retrieval Design Matter

The goal of this module is to guide you through building a robust, production-ready storage and retrieval system for multimodal data in e-commerce, such as customer photos, manuals, invoices, videos, and audio. The running example is TechVista's fashion and electronics store, where every upload has to remain searchable, trustworthy, and traceable end to end.

By the end, you will understand

  • How to layer raw blobs, processed vectors, and metadata across storage systems
  • Smart chunking strategies that preserve meaning across images, tables, documents, video, and audio

You will also understand

  • Essential metadata fields and lineage tracking for trust, debugging, and compliance
  • Index design choices and why metadata filtering matters at query time

This is what turns chaotic uploads into a reliable, filterable, auditable knowledge base that powers accurate multimodal RAG. You cannot just dump files into S3 and call it a day. Modern multimodal pipelines coordinate raw data, processed representations, and metadata together.

That coordination matters because production questions are rarely clean. One customer may ask for a battery comparison from a spec sheet, another may upload a cracked-screen photo and ask whether the damage is covered, and a support agent may need the latest manual rather than an outdated marketplace listing. Storage design, chunking discipline, metadata quality, and retrieval policy are what decide whether the answer is grounded, current, and safe to show.

02 — Layered Storage

How Production Storage Is Structured

A production pipeline stores raw objects, processed representations, and vector-indexed searchable forms as distinct but connected layers.

Multimodal Production Pipeline ANIMATED DATA FLOW
Layer 1

Raw Objects

JPEGs, PNGs, PDFs, videos, audio clips, OCR text, and transcripts live in cheap blob storage like S3, GCS, or Azure Blob.

Layer 2

Processed Representations

Embeddings, thumbnails, structured JSON, OCR output, bounding boxes, and table markdown sit alongside raw blobs.

Layer 3

Vector Database

Dense vectors plus payload metadata are stored in Pinecone, Weaviate, Qdrant, Milvus, Chroma, or pgvector.

Layer 4

Hybrid Retrieval

Dense vector search often works together with inverted text indexes for keyword fallback and ranking balance.

Layer 5

Grounded RAG

Query-time filtering, provenance, and pointers back to raw assets let the answer remain traceable and auditable.

Raw objects

  • Product photos and customer-uploaded damage images
  • PDF manuals, invoices, and spec sheets
  • 30–60 second video clips instead of giant full videos
  • Audio segments from voice notes or extracted video tracks
  • OCR text and ASR transcripts

Processed representations

  • Embeddings from CLIP, SigLIP, ImageBind, or ColPali
  • Thumbnails and previews
  • Bounding boxes from object detection
  • Structured extractions such as table markdown or JSON
  • Short BLIP-2 style captions paired with visual embeddings

Vector database payloads

  • Dense vectors
  • Metadata and filterable payloads
  • Pointers back to raw storage
  • Support for hybrid vector plus text retrieval
  • Fields filterable at query time for freshness, reliability, and compliance

Why These Layers Stay Separate in Production

Keeping these layers separate is not bureaucracy. It is what makes the system cheap, debuggable, and upgradeable. Raw blobs stay in low-cost durable storage. Processed views can be regenerated when OCR, captioning, or detection models improve. The vector database stays lean and query-focused, storing search-ready representations plus payloads rather than becoming a dumping ground for huge binary files.

This separation also protects immutability. The original evidence should remain untouched so you can always re-run OCR, re-caption an image, re-embed a document with a newer model, or re-check a compliance decision without losing the source of truth. That is the operational difference between a prototype and a system that can survive audits, model upgrades, and customer disputes.

  • Blob storage: cheapest place for original evidence, such as the untouched customer photo or original PDF manual.
  • Processed artifacts: OCR text, ASR transcript, thumbnails, table markdown, and detection JSON can be replaced without re-uploading the original file.
  • Vector index: should store the searchable representation and pointer, not the entire raw asset.
  • Metadata store: keeps richly filterable JSON that query logic, compliance checks, and debugging tools can all reuse.
Storage Choice

S3 / Blob Layer

Keep originals immutable so the system always has a source of truth.

Processing Choice

Derived Artifacts

OCR, captions, crops, previews, and structured JSON can evolve as models improve.

Retrieval Choice

Vector + Sparse Index

Keep retrieval fast, compact, and filterable instead of loading giant files at query time.

Trust Choice

Metadata + Lineage

Make every answer traceable back to when, where, and how the evidence was created.

Real-world example: Amazon product pages combine blob-stored images and PDF manuals with vector-indexed embeddings for semantic plus visual search such as “find similar wireless earbuds.”

03 — Chunking

Breaking Things Smartly Without Breaking Meaning

Chunking is not just “split every 512 tokens.” Different modalities require different chunking rules if you want retrieval to preserve meaning and context.

The key idea is that the chunk should match the unit of meaning. For a PDF manual, that might be a full page or region because the figure, caption, and nearby paragraph depend on one another. For a table, it might be one row plus its header. For a video, it is usually a scene or coherent time window rather than an arbitrary slice. When chunk boundaries ignore structure, retrieval may still find something relevant, but the generator loses the evidence needed to answer correctly.

Documents

  • Page-level chunking when layout matters
  • Region-level chunking via layout parsers such as Unstructured.io, Docling, or Nougat
  • Semantic chunking to avoid cutting related paragraphs apart

Tables

  • Preserve rows and columns using markdown or HTML
  • Chunk by row with header context for large tables
  • Optionally embed the table image and text representation together

Images

  • Use whole image for smaller inputs
  • Tile or crop very large product shots
  • Store both image vectors and generated captions
  • Use short BLIP-2 style captions so text and vision retrieval both stay available

Video

  • Extract keyframes every 2–5 seconds plus transcript
  • Use scene or shot detection for coherent segment boundaries

Audio

  • Use fixed time windows such as 10–30 seconds
  • Pair each chunk with transcript text
  • Apply speaker diarization when multiple voices matter

Chunking Trade-Offs by Modality

Documents

Best when: manuals, invoices, warranty documents, support PDFs.

Good strategy: region-based chunking by heading, paragraph, figure, and table.

Main risk: naive token splitting destroys layout meaning and breaks citations.

Tables

Best when: spec sheets, comparison tables, pricing matrices.

Good strategy: preserve headers with each row or logical section.

Main risk: split away the headers and the row values become meaningless.

Images

Best when: damage photos, product shots, screenshots.

Good strategy: whole-image for compact inputs, tile or crop only when scale demands it.

Main risk: over-cropping removes context, while under-cropping hides fine defects.

Video and Audio

Best when: unboxing clips, review videos, voice notes, support calls.

Good strategy: keyframe + transcript or fixed time windows plus speaker or scene boundaries.

Main risk: arbitrary cuts break causality, sequence, and who-said-what context.

In e-commerce, bad chunking can silently destroy answer quality. If a battery-comparison table gets split in the wrong place, the model may lose header context and give the wrong comparison. Good chunking keeps the row and header together so the answer stays grounded.

Real-world example: Weaviate-style semantic chunking improves retrieval from product manuals for questions like “How do I charge WH-1000XM5 headphones?”

The broader lesson is that chunking is a context-preservation problem, not a token-budget trick. In TechVista's pipeline, the correct chunk may be a full manual page, a table row plus headers, a product image plus caption, or a video scene paired with transcript text. The right unit is whatever preserves the answerable meaning.

This is why the semantic threshold matters in practice. A threshold like 0.75 is not just a number in code; it is the boundary between keeping two sentences together because they explain the same idea and splitting them because the topic has shifted. In production, teams tune that threshold by measuring whether citations stay coherent and whether answers stop dropping important nearby context.

Python · Semantic Chunking
def semantic_chunk(texts, embedder, threshold=0.75):
    embeddings = embedder.encode(texts)
    chunks = []
    current = [texts[0]]
    for i in range(1, len(texts)):
        sim = cosine_sim(embeddings[i-1], embeddings[i])
        if sim >= threshold:
            current.append(texts[i])
        else:
            chunks.append(" ".join(current))
            current = [texts[i]]
    chunks.append(" ".join(current))
    return chunks
Python · Structure-Preserving Table Chunking
def chunk_table(rows, header, max_rows=4):
    chunks = []
    for i in range(0, len(rows), max_rows):
        batch = rows[i:i + max_rows]
        chunk = {
            "header": header,           # keep schema context attached
            "rows": batch,              # preserve full row semantics
            "text_view": to_markdown(header, batch)
        }
        chunks.append(chunk)
    return chunks
04 — Metadata and Lineage

The Who, When, Why, and How of Every Chunk

Without metadata, you are flying blind. With rich metadata, your system knows where a chunk came from, how fresh it is, how trustworthy it is, how it was processed, and whether it contains sensitive information.

The deeper lesson is that metadata is not a side table for administrators. It is active retrieval logic. The retriever uses it to filter stale sources, ranking uses it to reward trusted evidence, the UI uses it to cite provenance, and compliance logic uses it to decide whether content should be hidden, blurred, or redacted.

Five Essential Metadata Fields

Provenance

Stores original source and unique identifier.

  • Manufacturer manual: "source": "manufacturer_sony.com/manuals/WH-1000XM5_2025-03.pdf" and "crawl_date": "2025-03-15"
  • Customer upload: "source": "customer_upload", "upload_id": "CU-987654", "user_id": "user_44521"
  • Review video: "source": "youtube_review" and "video_id": "dQw4w9wgabc"

Timestamps

Use multiple dates because freshness has more than one meaning.

  • ingestion_timestamp: when the file entered your pipeline
  • last_updated: when the source owner last changed it
  • content_creation_date: when the customer photo or source artifact was originally created

Reliability Score

A simple 1–10 trust signal your retriever can filter or rerank on.

  • 10 = official manufacturer manual, manually verified
  • 9 = verified product spec from brand API
  • 7 = YouTube unboxing video from verified channel
  • 6 = customer-uploaded photo that may be edited or incomplete
  • 4 = auto-crawled marketplace listing that may be outdated
  • 2 = random forum post

Processing History

Full audit trail of every transformation: OCR model, chunking strategy, embedding model, and so on.

"ocr_model": "TrOCR-v2", "strategy": "region_based", and embedding model pointers are the kind of fields that make debugging possible.

PII / Sensitivity Flags

Marks whether content includes faces, serial numbers, or other sensitive information and what action was taken.

Example fields: pii_detected, action_taken, and sensitivity_level. For example: face visible in a customer photo, serial number visible on a laptop, or invoice address requiring redaction.

Lineage is the directed graph that shows how a chunk traveled from raw input to final index entry. It answers the question: how did this exact piece of content end up in my retrieval system?

This is the same style of lineage graph you see in tools such as Collibra, Amundsen, or open-source DataHub: nodes represent assets, arrows represent transformations, and branches show how one source can feed both text and multimodal paths.

In TechVista's system, a single broken MacBook photo can travel through several parallel branches: raw photo in S3, face blurring for compliance, OCR for serial number extraction, object detection for crack localization, caption generation for text-side retrieval, multimodal embedding for visual similarity, and finally a hybrid index entry that joins those outputs together. That full graph is what lets an engineer answer, with evidence, why a support response was right or wrong.

Step-by-Step Metadata + Lineage Process

  1. Ingestion trigger: a photo or manual lands in the raw bucket after a webhook fires and immediately gets initial provenance and upload timestamp.
  2. Metadata enrichment: a lightweight processor adds provenance, timestamps, reliability score, and PII scan results using services such as AWS Rekognition or DeepPrivacy.
  3. Processing pipeline: OCR, chunking, embedding, and every other step append to metadata history. This can run in Kafka or an Airflow DAG.
  4. Lineage graph construction: each step emits an event to a lineage service so the full graph can be rebuilt automatically.
  5. Storage: raw files stay in blob storage, metadata and lineage JSON live in MongoDB or PostgreSQL JSONB, and vectors plus payloads go into a vector DB.
  6. Query-time usage: the retriever can now filter by reliability, date, or PII status, or skip and blur sensitive results before they are shown.
  7. Auditing and debugging: support teams can click a chunk ID, open the lineage trace, and see when a bad answer came from an outdated or low-reliability source.

This step-by-step process is what makes metadata operational instead of decorative. It is not enough to store fields. Those fields must be written during ingest, updated during every transformation, queryable during retrieval, and visible during audits.

05 — Index Design and Retrieval

Where Retrieval Quality Is Won or Lost

Index design determines how your system actually retrieves evidence. In production, most systems no longer choose between keyword search and vector search. They combine them.

That combination is not academic. Real user queries often mix exact constraints, fuzzy intent, and multimodal evidence. "Find similar wireless earbuds" leans heavily on semantic and visual similarity. "WH-1000XM5 charging issue" needs exact product naming. "Is this damage covered?" may require warranty text, image evidence, and metadata filters all at once.

Index Design Comparison RETRIEVAL STRATEGIES
Text Index Only

BM25 / Elasticsearch

Strong keyword fallback. Good for exact terms, model names, serial numbers, and error codes, but weak on semantic similarity and visual matching.

Vector Index Only

Pure Semantic Search

Dense retrieval using ANN structures such as HNSW in systems like Pinecone or Qdrant. Strong on meaning, but exact keyword constraints can get lost without sparse support.

Hybrid / Multimodal

Dense + Sparse Together

Combine vector similarity and BM25, often with reciprocal rank fusion or weighted sum scoring. This is the production default.

The source also calls out vector database internals such as sharding, replication, and indexing. You do not need those internals to understand retrieval quality yet, but they are what make distributed multimodal search scale in production.

In practice, those internals affect latency and reliability. Sharding spreads vectors across machines so large catalogs remain searchable. Replication keeps the system available when one node fails. ANN structures such as HNSW make approximate nearest-neighbor search fast enough that hybrid retrieval can still feel interactive for a customer-facing assistant.

Multimodal pair indices can store image-text pairs together so a query like “wireless earbuds with ANC” retrieves both product photos and spec text. Filtering matters just as much as retrieval style.

How Production Teams Usually Decide

  • Text-only index: keep it as a fallback for exact SKU queries, error messages, or legal phrasing.
  • Vector-only index: useful when the main problem is semantic similarity, image matching, or open-ended language retrieval.
  • Hybrid index: the production default because many user questions mix exact terms with fuzzy meaning.
  • Multimodal pair index: critical when one query should retrieve both text evidence and visual evidence together.
Query-Time Filters
reliability ≥ 8   ·   content_creation_date > 2025-01-01   ·   pii_detected = false
Metadata filters are how you keep results trustworthy, fresh, and compliant. Retrieval quality is not just similarity score. It is score plus policy.

Real-world example: Weaviate or Pinecone hybrid search can combine keyword and vector signals for a query like “ANC earbuds under $200.”

Python · Weighted Hybrid Ranking
def hybrid_score(vector_score, bm25_score, reliability):
    # Dense meaning + sparse exact match + trust signal
    return 0.55 * vector_score + 0.30 * bm25_score + 0.15 * (reliability / 10.0)

query = "Compare battery life of these three phones from the spec sheet"

# Example outcome: table row with header context outranks a generic paragraph
scored = sorted(candidates, key=lambda x: hybrid_score(
    x["vector_score"], x["bm25_score"], x["metadata"]["reliability"]
), reverse=True)

The point of a weighting scheme like 0.55 / 0.30 / 0.15 is not that these exact numbers are universal. It is that production teams explicitly decide how much semantic similarity, exact lexical match, and trustworthiness should influence ranking. Those weights are tuned against the failure modes the business cares about most: wrong product comparisons, bad support answers, or unsafe content exposure.

This is why hybrid retrieval wins so often in production. A query like "Is this damage covered?" may require exact warranty language, semantic understanding of the support intent, and image evidence from a customer-uploaded photo. No single index type handles all of that well on its own.

06 — Conclusion and Assessment

Key Takeaways from Module 5

A production multimodal pipeline coordinates cheap raw storage, vector databases, smart chunking, rich metadata, and lineage tracking into one reliable retrieval system.

Layer storage: raw blobs, vectors, and metadata should be coordinated, not mixed blindly.

Chunk modality-smart: region/layout for documents and tables, keyframe + transcript for video.

Metadata essentials: provenance, timestamps, reliability, processing history, and PII flags.

Lineage graphs let you debug why a wrong answer happened instead of guessing.

Hybrid filtered retrieval gives fresher, higher-quality, more compliant results.

In e-commerce, this leads to accurate battery comparisons, grounded damage assessments, compliant PII handling, fewer returns, and more trust.

In product terms, the recipe is clear: keep raw evidence cheap and durable, preserve meaning while chunking, attach metadata that retrieval can actually filter on, and make lineage visible enough that a wrong answer can be traced back to the exact source, transformation, and policy decision that produced it.

In e-commerce terms, that translates directly into business outcomes: fewer returns because comparisons stay grounded, faster support resolution because evidence is traceable, and lower compliance risk because sensitive content can be filtered or redacted before it reaches a customer-facing answer.
