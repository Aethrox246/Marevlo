02 — Chatbot

What a Normal AI Chatbot Really Is

Imagine opening ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini and typing Tell me about the battery life of the Galaxy Watch 7. The system reads your message, thinks for a moment, gives a fluent answer, and then stops to wait for the next instruction. That is the basic chatbot pattern.

Can Do
  • Understand words very well
  • Write fluent, helpful answers
  • Remember the conversation for a few turns
Cannot Do
  • See photos or screenshots
  • Open websites or click buttons
  • Fill forms or take actions in the outside world
  • Plan multiple steps and execute them autonomously

The important word is passive. A chatbot reacts when you speak, but it does not go out and do work. The source compares it to a very smart assistant who can only talk on the phone. Helpful, yes. Capable of taking action, no.

This is where many people stop mentally. They assume “AI assistant” means “agent,” but a normal chatbot is still mostly a text responder, not a goal-seeking worker.

03 — Agent

What Changes When a Chatbot Becomes an AI Agent

An AI agent is an AI system that does not just answer questions. It actively works to achieve a goal like a human employee would. Instead of only responding, it can plan the work, use tools, observe results, and continue until the task is finished.

The Three Superpowers

Planning

Break a large goal into smaller logical steps and decide the right order.

Tool Use

Use external tools such as search, databases, websites, photo analysis, forms, or email systems.

Looping

Do one step, inspect the result, learn from it, and decide what to do next until the goal is achieved.

Simple Analogy
chatbot = smart librarian    ·    agent = personal assistant
The librarian tells you where the book is. The assistant goes, gets it, highlights what matters, and can even mail it to you.

TechVista example: if a customer says My Galaxy Watch 7 battery is draining too fast, a chatbot gives a general explanation. A real AI agent can analyze the specific case, check the exact model, look up known issues, open the support system, and even start a return request if the evidence supports it.

04 — Multimodal Agent

What a Multimodal Agent Adds That Text Agents Cannot

A multimodal agent is an AI agent that has eyes, and sometimes ears. While regular text agents understand words, multimodal agents can understand and work with several kinds of information at the same time.

Text

Written questions, typed instructions, chat history, and policies.

Images

Uploaded damage photos, product pictures, and inspection snapshots.

Screenshots

What a website or app currently looks like during a workflow.

Documents

PDF manuals, invoices, warranty rules, tables, and spec sheets.

Video and Audio

Unboxing clips, voice notes, support recordings, and spoken evidence.

This is revolutionary because most real business problems are visual. Customers do not only say my watch is broken. They upload a photo. Support people do not only read text. They inspect the product image, the serial number, the warranty table, and the claim form itself. A multimodal agent can literally see all of that and then act on it.

The difference is not cosmetic. A text-only agent can read your sentence about a cracked watch. A multimodal agent can inspect the crack, read the serial number, verify the policy table, and then decide whether to initiate the claim.

TechVista example: you upload a cracked smartwatch photo and ask Is this covered under warranty? A normal text agent gives a generic answer. A multimodal agent sees the crack, reads the serial number, finds the exact policy, checks the damage type, and replies with a grounded answer such as: Yes, this matches a manufacturing defect pattern and I have started the claim for you.

05 — Agent Loop

The Five-Step Agent Loop That Makes Agents Feel Alive

Every multimodal agent, whether single-agent or multi-agent, follows a repeating cycle. This loop is what turns a static model into a system that can perceive, plan, act, verify, and continue until the goal is actually achieved.

Animated Agent Loop PERCEPTION → PLANNING → TOOLS → VERIFICATION → ACTION
Step 1

Perception

Receive text, photos, screenshots, or documents and understand what is actually present.

Step 2

Planning

Create a strategy for solving the user’s goal in the right order.

Step 3

Tool Calling

Use image-analysis, document, UI, or database tools to execute the plan.

Step 4

Verification

Double-check that the conclusion actually matches the evidence before acting.

Step 5

Response or Action

Deliver a grounded answer or perform the external action, then loop again if needed.

Step-by-Step With the TechVista Warranty Claim

  1. Perception: the agent sees the uploaded smartwatch photo and understands: Galaxy Watch 7, visible hairline crack on the upper-right side, serial number visible, likely about 4 months old.
  2. Planning: it decides to confirm the exact model, find the warranty policy, check whether that damage type is covered, and then help file the claim if eligible.
  3. Tool Calling: it uses an image-analysis tool, a document-retrieval tool, a database tool, and potentially a UI-navigation tool.
  4. Verification: it checks whether the conclusion matches both the photo and the policy document so it does not hallucinate or take the wrong action.
  5. Response or Action: it either gives a grounded answer with proof or submits the claim and sends confirmation.

The verification step is what keeps an agent from becoming dangerously confident. Without it, the system can take action based on a wrong perception or a misread policy.

In implementation terms, verification is often a reflection or critique pass. The agent asks a second question before acting: Does the conclusion match the visible evidence, the retrieved policy, and the tool outputs? If the answer is no, it should loop back, retrieve more context, ask a clarifying question, or escalate to a human instead of pushing forward blindly.

Python · Simple ReAct-Style Agent Loop
state = {"goal": user_request, "evidence": [], "done": False}

while not state["done"]:
    thought = plan_next_step(state)
    action = choose_tool_or_response(thought)

    if action["type"] == "tool":
        result = call_tool(action["name"], action["args"])
        state["evidence"].append(result)
        critique = verify_against_goal(state, result)
        if critique["needs_more_work"]:
            state["goal"] = critique["refined_goal"]
            continue
    else:
        state["final_response"] = action["content"]
        state["done"] = True
06 — Single vs Multi-Agent

When One Agent Is Enough and When a Team Works Better

Single-Agent System

One powerful multimodal model handles the entire loop by itself.

  • Advantages: simpler to build, simpler to deploy, fast for straightforward tasks
  • Best for: quick warranty checks or simple customer-service flows
  • Disadvantage: one model has to be expert at everything and can get overwhelmed on complex tasks
Multi-Agent System

Several specialized agents collaborate like a real human team.

  • Advantages: each agent becomes very good at one specialty
  • Best for: long, complex tasks with several kinds of evidence and actions
  • Disadvantage: much more complex to coordinate and build
Perception Agent

Expert at reading photos, screenshots, and videos.

Document Agent

Expert at PDFs, tables, manuals, and policy pages.

UI Agent

Expert at navigating websites, clicking buttons, and filling forms.

Verification Agent

Expert at catching mistakes and checking consistency.

Coordinator Agent

Assigns work, collects results, and decides the next step.

Architectural Patterns in Practice

Supervisor Pattern

One orchestrator agent routes tasks to specialists, collects outputs, resolves conflicts, and decides when the case is ready for response or action.

Hierarchical Routing

A senior planner creates the big-picture plan while lower-level agents handle narrow subproblems like OCR, photo inspection, or UI form filling.

Agent Communication

Agents often exchange structured messages rather than raw prose: intent, evidence, confidence, and next-action recommendations.

The source’s rule of thumb is practical: in 2026, companies use both. Simple queries often go through one agent for speed. Complex support cases involving photo analysis, policy lookup, form filling, and verification are much better handled by a coordinated multi-agent team.

07 — Planning Styles and Tools

How Agents Decide What to Do and How They Act on Visual Inputs

Planning Styles

Reactive Planning

Also called ReAct. The agent does one step, sees the result, then immediately decides the next step. Flexible and strong for dynamic situations.

Planner-Executor

Create a full step-by-step plan first, then follow it. More organized and better for longer workflows.

Hierarchical Planning

A senior agent makes the big-picture plan and delegates details to junior agents. Best for complex real-world work.

These planning styles are not just labels. ReAct-style loops are useful when the environment changes as the agent works. Planner-executor systems are better when the task is predictable and long. Hierarchical planning is strongest when there are several different tools, several different evidence types, and enough complexity that one model should not juggle everything alone.

Visual Tool Use

The biggest difference between text agents and multimodal agents is visual tool use. The agent is not only sending words to tools. It can send photos, screenshots, or document pages and get structured results back.

Image Analysis Tool

Can measure crack severity, identify defect patterns, or detect visible damage type.

Document Retrieval Tool

Finds the exact page, table row, or policy section inside a warranty PDF.

UI Navigation Tool

Opens the claim portal, clicks the right buttons, and fills the correct fields.

Database Tool

Checks serial numbers, purchase history, and account records.

Example outputs from these tools make the power concrete: the damage-analysis tool can return Crack detected on upper right, severity high, defect pattern #47, while a UI tool can inspect a screenshot of the warranty website and click the correct action button automatically.

JSON · Tool Schema With Image Input
{
  "tool": "analyze_damage_photo",
  "inputs": {
    "image_path": "customer_uploads/watch_4471.jpg",
    "task": "detect_damage_and_extract_serial"
  },
  "expected_output": {
    "damage_type": "string",
    "location": "string",
    "severity": "string",
    "serial_number": "string",
    "confidence": "float"
  }
}
Python · Visual Tool Call and Output Parsing
photo_result = analyze_damage_photo(
    image="watch_4471.jpg",
    task="detect_damage_and_extract_serial"
)

# Example parsed output:
# {
#   "damage_type": "screen_crack",
#   "location": "upper_right",
#   "severity": "high",
#   "serial_number": "XYZ123",
#   "confidence": 0.94
# }

if photo_result["confidence"] < 0.8:
    escalate_to_human_review()

Structured tool outputs matter because they give the next step something reliable to work with. Instead of passing around vague prose, the agent can route specific fields like severity = high or serial_number = XYZ123 into policy lookup, UI filling, or verification logic.

This is the real revolution in multimodal agents. They do not only understand what the customer typed. They can see what the customer showed, inspect what the website looks like, and then take the next correct action inside the digital environment.

Production pitfall: never let a tool output directly trigger a sensitive action without checks. Visual tools can be uncertain, screenshots can be stale, and UI pages can change. Good agents validate confidence, re-check critical facts, and escalate risky cases instead of auto-submitting blindly.

08 — Warranty Claim Walkthrough

How the Same Task Looks in Single-Agent and Multi-Agent Form

Single-Agent Version

One strong multimodal agent receives the cracked watch photo, analyzes the damage, finds the warranty policy, checks eligibility, opens the claim form, fills the required fields, and sends a confirmation. This is ideal when the case is clear and the workflow is short.

  1. Observe: detect screen crack, extract serial number, identify likely model as Galaxy Watch 7.
  2. Retrieve: fetch purchase record and warranty policy page for that model.
  3. Decide: compare defect pattern with known manufacturing issue rules.
  4. Act: prefill claim form and prepare customer-facing explanation.
  5. Verify: confirm that the crack location and policy match before submitting.

Multi-Agent Version

  • Perception Agent: analyzes the photo and extracts the serial number
  • Document Agent: finds the exact warranty rules and highlights the relevant row
  • UI Agent: opens the claim portal and pre-fills the form
  • Verification Agent: checks everything against the photo and the policy
  • Response Agent: sends a grounded message with annotated images and clear next steps

Failure modes become easier to isolate in the multi-agent setup. If the perception agent misreads the serial number, the issue is not in policy reasoning. If the UI agent fails to submit the form, the perception and policy steps may still be correct. That isolation is one of the biggest practical reasons teams adopt multi-agent designs for harder workflows.

This is the direct leap from a helpful support bot to an autonomous digital employee. The multimodal agent does not only explain the warranty. It sees your cracked watch, reads the policy, fills the form, and starts the claim.

Business impact is where this becomes real. A strong multimodal claim agent can reduce resolution time, lower support cost, cut disputes, and improve customer satisfaction because the decision is grounded in what the customer actually showed instead of a generic script.

09 — Production Reality

What Real Companies Have to Worry About Beyond the Demo

Cost and Latency

Vision calls, document parsing, and UI navigation are expensive. Good systems only invoke the heavy tools when needed.

Error Recovery

If a tool fails or a page changes, the agent should retry, replan, or escalate rather than inventing progress.

Human-in-the-Loop

Risky cases like ambiguous damage, low-confidence vision output, or expensive refunds should route to a human reviewer.

Observability

Teams need trace logs of thoughts, tool calls, outputs, and decisions so failures are debuggable, similar to LangSmith-style traces.

Safety Guardrails

Agents need action limits, confidence thresholds, and policy checks before sending emails, issuing refunds, or submitting claims.

This is the production difference between an impressive demo and a deployable system. Real agents need bounded autonomy: enough freedom to solve the task, but enough controls to prevent bad actions, runaway loops, and silent errors.
