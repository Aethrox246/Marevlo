Module 1 — Introduction to Document AI and Document Structure

Goal: Get you super clear on what Document AI actually is, why it’s so tricky, and why understanding document structure is the secret sauce that makes everything work.

What is Document AI?

Document AI is basically AI that can “read” any document like a human expert, pulling out important info, classifying what kind of document it is, spotting tables, titles, or even reading faded old scans. Think of it as the smart assistant that turns mountains of paperwork into clean, usable data in seconds.

It powers stuff like auto-filling invoice totals, sorting resumes for HR, or digitizing old government files. Without good Document AI, companies waste hours (and lakhs of rupees) on manual work.

Why documents are difficult for machines

Machines are great at photos of cats or cars, but documents? Total nightmare. Why?

- Documents have super dense text, tiny fonts, and crazy layouts that change every time.

- Scanned pages can be blurry, rotated, faded, or have coffee stains (true story from Bhopal offices!).

- A machine sees just pixels, no clue that a bold line at the top is a “title” or that numbers in boxes are a “table”.

- One small shift in format (like a new bank logo) and the model gets totally confused.

That’s why regular computer vision models fail hard on documents.

Difference between plain text, structured documents, and scanned documents

Let’s keep it simple, bro:

- Plain text → Just words in a .txt file. No layout, no images, super easy for computers. Example: a simple email body.

- Structured documents → Like PDFs or Word files with perfect digital layout (titles, tables, everything in order). Machines can extract text easily, but they still miss “where” things are on the page.

- Scanned documents → Real-world killer. These are photos of paper (invoice scans, old forms, handwritten notes). No text layer at all — just pixels. This is where most Indian offices live (Bhopal shops, banks, courts). Here you need full vision AI to first “see” the layout before you can read anything.

Common document elements

Every document is made of the same building blocks:

- Title: - Big bold text at the top (e.g., “INVOICE #12345”)

- Paragraph: - Normal body text blocks

- Table: -Grid of rows and columns (prices, quantities, totals)

- Figure: -Charts, logos, photos, or diagrams

- Header/Footer: - Page numbers, company name, date at top or bottom

If the AI can spot these elements correctly, everything else (extraction, classification, search) becomes easy.

Examples of real documents

- Invoice → The shopkeeper in New Market, Bhopal scans his daily sales invoice. It has a big title, item table, total box, and GST footer. One wrong table detection and the accounts guy spend hours fixing totals.

- Resume → A fresh engineering graduate from MANIT Bhopal sends his resume. AI needs to find name (title), education (table), skills (paragraph), and photo (figure) to auto-sort it for HR.

- Research paper→ A professor at Barkatullah University submits a scanned PDF. Title on top, abstract paragraph, big tables of results, figures of graphs, and page numbers in footer.

How humans understand documents

You and I glance at a page for 2 seconds and instantly know:

“This bold part is the title… this grid is a table… that number at the bottom is the total.”

We use visual cues (bold, borders, spacing, alignment) + common sense. Machines have zero common sense, they need to be taught these visual rules from scratch. That’s exactly what document layout understanding does.

High-level idea of document layout understanding

It’s the first and most important step in any Document AI system:

1. Take the image/scan

2. Detect where every element is (bounding boxes)

3. Classify what each box is (title, table, paragraph, etc.)

4. Then only read the text inside those boxes (OCR) or extract data.

Get this step right → the whole pipeline becomes accurate and fast. Mess it up → everything downstream fails.

Module 2: OCR Fundamentals

Goal: Understand exactly how machines pull text out of document images and why OCR alone is never enough for real Document AI.

What OCR is?

OCR stands for Optical Character Recognition.

It’s the magic that takes a photo or scan of a page and turns every printed (or handwritten) character into real, copy-paste-able text.

Without OCR, your scanned invoice is just a picture. With OCR, it becomes editable data you can put in Excel or feed to ChatGPT. Super important for shops, banks, and government offices in India.

OCR pipeline, two simple steps

Every OCR system follows the same two-step dance:

1. Text Detection

Finds WHERE the text is on the page.

It draws a box (called a bounding box) around every word or line.

2. Text Recognition

Looks inside each box and actually reads the letters/numbers.

“Total Amount: ₹1,250” → outputs the actual string “Total Amount: ₹1,250”.

That’s it! Detection first, then recognition.

How OCR reads documents

OCR doesn’t “understand” like we do. It looks for:

- Sharp contrast between text and background

- Edges of letters

- Patterns it has seen during training

It works great on clean printed text but starts crying when the paper is faded, tilted, or has coffee stains (very common in Bhopal kirana shops!).

Bounding boxes in OCR

A bounding box is just four numbers:

`(x1, y1, x2, y2)` — left-top corner and right-bottom corner of the box.

Example from an invoice:

- Box around “INVOICE #12345” → title area

- Box around each row in the item table → price columns

These boxes are gold because they tell you the position of every piece of text.

Example OCR output

When you run OCR, you usually get something like this:

json

[

["INVOICE", [120, 80, 380, 120], 0.98],

["Date: 04-Mar-2026", [450, 85, 720, 110], 0.95],

["Item", [50, 220, 150, 240], 0.99],

["Qty", [200, 220, 250, 240], 0.97],

["Amount", [400, 220, 520, 240], 0.96],

["Rice", [50, 260, 150, 280], 0.93],

["10 kg", [200, 260, 260, 280], 0.94],

["₹850", [400, 260, 480, 280], 0.91]

]

Each line = [text, bounding box coordinates, confidence score]

Running OCR on a document image

Here’s a super simple, runnable code using EasyOCR (works great on Indian documents and supports Hindi too):

python

from PIL import Image

import easyocr

import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

import matplotlib.patches as patches

# Load your scanned document (works on invoice, resume, anything)

img = Image.open('bhopal_invoice_scan.jpg')

# Create OCR reader (English + Hindi for Indian docs)

reader = easyocr.Reader(['en', 'hi'], gpu=False) # set gpu=True if you have one

# Run OCR

results = reader.readtext('bhopal_invoice_scan.jpg', detail=1)

print("=== OCR OUTPUT ===")

for (bbox, text, confidence) in results:

print(f"Text: {text} | Confidence: {confidence:.2f} | Box: {bbox}")

Visualizing OCR results (so you can actually SEE the boxes)

python

fig, ax = plt.subplots(1, figsize=(12, 16))

plt.title("OCR Results on Bhopal Invoice Scan")

plt.axis('off')

plt.show()

Limitations of OCR for structured documents

Here’s the big truth, bro, OCR only gives you flat text + boxes. It has zero idea about structure.

- It cannot tell you “this text is inside a table cell”

- It cannot separate header from footer

- It mixes up everything when tables have merged cells or lines

- On a messy scanned invoice, it might read “Total 1250” but won’t know it belongs to the grand total box

- Handwritten parts or low-quality scans drop accuracy fast

Real-world example:

A grocery shop scans daily invoices. Plain OCR reads all numbers correctly but cannot tell which amount is “Rice 10kg ₹850” vs “Oil 5L ₹420” because it doesn’t understand the table grid. You still need a human to organize it. That’s exactly why we need Document Layout Analysis (DiT style) on top of OCR!

Module 3: The Need for Layout Detection

Goal: Understand why just reading text with OCR is not enough and why we need a smart “layout detector” to make Document AI actually useful in real life.

Problem with OCR-only systems

OCR is awesome at reading words, but it treats every document like a flat page full of random text. It spits out hundreds of words with boxes, but has zero understanding of what those words mean in the bigger picture.

Result? Total chaos on real documents.

You get correct text, but no clue:

- Which part is the title?

- Where does the table start and end?

- Which numbers belong to the total?

In a Bhopal kirana shop, the owner scans 50 invoices a day. OCR reads every rupee amount correctly… but mixes up item prices with GST and grand total. The accountant still has to sit and fix everything manually. Waste of time and money!

Examples of messy OCR output

Here’s what plain OCR gives you on a typical scanned invoice (real example from a New Market shop in Bhopal):

INVOICE

Date 04-03-2026

Item Qty Rate Amount

Rice 10kg 85 850

Oil 5L 84 420

Total 1270

GST 18% 228.6

Grand Total 1498.6

Thank you!

Looks okay, right? But wait, OCR actually outputs this mess:

["INVOICE", box1], ["Date", box2], ["04-03-2026", box3], ["Item", box4], ["Qty", box5], ["Rice", box6], ["10kg", box7], ["85", box8], ["850", box9]...

No grouping. No idea that “Rice”, “10kg”, “85”, “850” belong together in one row.

The table is completely broken. Welcome to real-world pain.

Concept of document regions

Think of a document like a house.

- Rooms = different regions (title room, table room, footer room)

- Walls = clear separations (lines, spacing, bold text)

Machines need to first find these “rooms” (regions) before they can live in them properly.

That’s exactly what layout detection does.

What layout detection means

Layout detection = teaching the AI to look at the page and say:

“Here is a big bold TITLE region”

“Here is a TABLE region with 5 rows”

“Here is a FIGURE (photo or chart)”

“Here is a PARAGRAPH block”

It draws proper bounding boxes around these meaningful groups — not just single words.

Difference between text detection and layout detection

Let’s make it crystal clear, bro:

Thing

Text Detection (OCR Step 1)

Layout Detection

What it finds

Every single word or line

Big meaningful regions

Output

Hundreds of tiny boxes

Few big boxes (Title, Table, etc.)

Example

Box around “Rice”, box around “850”

One big box around the entire table

Usefulness

Good for plain reading

Required for structure & understanding

Text detection is like reading every letter on a page.

Layout detection is like understanding the whole newspaper layout.

Treating document regions as objects

Here’s the smart trick:

We treat each document region exactly like an “object” in normal photos.

- A table = a car

- A title = a person

- A figure = a traffic sign

So we use the same object detection technology (the same one that detects dogs in photos) — but now for document parts!

That’s why models like DiT (which we’ll build later) are so powerful.

Real-world example:

A bank receives 1000 scanned statements daily. Layout detection finds the “table” region first → extracts only transaction rows → auto-updates customer balances. No more manual copy-paste!

Module 4 — How Layout Detection Models Work

Goal: See exactly how a layout detection model turns a messy scanned document into clean, labeled regions, just like the DiT models we’ll build later.

Object detection basics

Layout detection is just object detection… but for documents! Instead of finding cars or dogs in a photo, the model finds “objects” like titles, tables, and paragraphs on a page. It looks at the entire image, predicts where each important region is, and gives it a name. Super simple idea, but it changes everything.

Bounding box prediction

The model doesn’t just guess, it predicts four numbers for every region:(x1, y1, x2, y2)`, exactly like in OCR, but now for big meaningful blocks. It also predicts a confidence score (how sure it is) and sometimes a mask (pixel-perfect shape for tables).

Real Bhopal example: On a scanned grocery invoice, the model predicts one big box around the entire item table so you can extract rows easily, no more guessing from single-word boxes.

Region classification

After finding the box, the model says “what is inside this box?”

It classifies it into categories like:

- Title

- Paragraph

- Table

- Figure

- List

- Header/Footer

This is called region classification. One model does both box prediction + classification in one go.

Input and output of layout detection models

Input: Just a single image (scanned page resized to 224×224 or higher). No text needed — pure vision!

Output: A clean list of regions, something like this:

json

[

{"bbox": [120, 80, 680, 140], "label": "Title", "score": 0.98},

{"bbox": [50, 200, 720, 520], "label": "Table", "score": 0.95},

{"bbox": [450, 80, 720, 110], "label": "Header", "score": 0.92},

{"bbox": [100, 580, 650, 620], "label": "Footer", "score": 0.89}

]

Each entry = one clean, labeled block. That’s gold for downstream tasks!

Layout detection pipeline (step-by-step)

Here’s exactly what happens inside the model:

1. Take scanned image → resize

2. Run through the backbone (DiT/ViT in our case) → get rich features

3. Predict possible boxes + classes (using Faster R-CNN or Cascade R-CNN head)

4. Remove bad predictions (Non-Maximum Suppression)

5. Output the final clean regions

Super-fast, runs in milliseconds on a normal laptop.

Module 5 — Layout Detection with YOLOv8

Goal: Get your hands dirty with a super-practical, ready-to-run layout detector using YOLOv8 — the fastest and easiest way to start detecting titles, tables, and paragraphs today.

Why YOLO works well for document layout detection

YOLO (You Only Look Once) is perfect for documents.

Why?

- It’s blazing fast (real-time on a normal laptop)

- Handles small objects like tiny page numbers or thin table lines really well

- Easy to train on custom document datasets

- One model does everything: finds the box + gives the label (Title/Table/Figure) in a single pass

In Bhopal shops and banks, where you scan 100 invoices a day, speed matters. YOLO gives results in 10–20 milliseconds per page, perfect for production use before we go deep into DiT later.

YOLO detection intuition

Think of YOLO like this:

It divides the whole page into a grid (like 80×80 tiny cells).

For every cell it asks two simple questions:

1. Is there a document region (title/table/etc.) in this area?

2. If yes, where exactly are the four corners of the box and what type is it?

It predicts everything at once, no complicated multi-stage pipeline. That’s why it’s called “You Only Look Once”!

Using existing layout datasets (PubLayNet etc)

We don’t need to label anything ourselves. We use the same datasets from Module 4:

- PubLayNet (360k scientific papers)

- DocBank (500k+ business docs)

- DocLayNet (real messy scans, best for Indian invoices)

These come with ready bounding boxes and labels. The only thing we do is convert them to YOLO format (a simple .txt file per image with class + box coordinates). There are free scripts online, takes 10 minutes.

YOLO training workflow

Super simple 4-step flow:

1. Prepare dataset in YOLO format (images + labels folder)

2. Create a small YAML file telling YOLO where the data is and what the 7 classes are

3. Train with one command

4. Test on your own scanned documents

Training YOLOv8 for document layouts

Here’s the exact code you can run today (tested on Google Colab or local machine):

python

# 1. Install YOLOv8 (one time)

!pip install ultralytics

from ultralytics import YOLO

# 2. Create data.yaml (save this file)

data_yaml = """

path: /content/document_layout_dataset # your folder

train: images/train

val: images/val

names:

0: Title

1: Paragraph

2: Table

3: Figure

4: List

5: Header

6: Footer

"""

with open('data.yaml', 'w') as f:

f.write(data_yaml)

# 3. Train the model (this is the magic command)

model = YOLO('yolov8s.pt') # start with small model for speed

model.train(

data='data.yaml',

epochs=50, # 20-50 is enough for good results

imgsz=640, # good for documents

batch=16,

name='dit_layout_yolov8',

pretrained=True

)

print("Training finished! Model saved in runs/detect/dit_layout_yolov8/")

After training you get `best.pt`, your own document layout detector!

Running inference on documents

Super easy inference code (use your scanned Bhopal invoice):

python

from ultralytics import YOLO

Module 6 — Advanced Layout Detection with Detectron2

Goal: Level up from the quick-and-easy YOLOv8 to the professional research-grade tool that top papers (including the original DiT paper) use, Detectron2. Now you’ll see how serious Document AI systems are built.

Introduction to Detectron2

Detectron2 is a super-powerful library made by Meta (Facebook AI). It’s like a complete toolbox for building object detection models. While YOLO is fast and simple, Detectron2 is the one researchers and big companies reach for when they want maximum accuracy. You can plug in any backbone (ViT, ResNet, or even our DiT later), add masks, and tweak everything easily.

Why Detectron2 is widely used in research

Here’s why almost every new document AI paper uses it, bro:

- Gives higher accuracy than most other tools

- Built-in support for advanced tricks like pixel-perfect masks

- Easy to swap backbones (exactly what the DiT paper did)

- Tons of ready configs and great community support

- Perfect for fair comparisons in research

In short: If you want results that can be published or used in real banks and law firms, Detectron2 is the gold standard.

Detectron2 detection architectures

Detectron2 makes two powerful models super easy to use:

- Faster R-CNN

Two-stage model: first finds possible regions, then refines the boxes and labels.

Fast enough and very accurate for documents.

- Mask R-CNN

Same as Faster R-CNN + extra magic: it also predicts exact pixel masks (perfect shape of tables, figures, etc.).

Amazing for messy invoices where tables have merged cells.

(The original DiT paper used an improved version called Cascade R-CNN with Detectron2 and crushed the benchmarks.)

Training a layout detection model

Here’s the clean, real training code (just copy-paste and run):

python

import os

from detectron2.config import get_cfg

from detectron2.engine import DefaultTrainer

cfg = get_cfg()

# Start with a strong base config

cfg.merge_from_file("configs/COCO-Detection/faster_rcnn_R_50_FPN_3x.yaml")

# Point to your data

cfg.DATASETS.TRAIN = ("publaynet_train",)

cfg.DATASETS.TEST = ("publaynet_val",)

cfg.DATALOADER.NUM_WORKERS = 4

# Use ImageNet weights or your own DiT backbone later

cfg.MODEL.WEIGHTS = "detectron2://ImageNetPretrained/MSRA/R-50.pkl"

# Settings perfect for documents

cfg.SOLVER.IMS_PER_BATCH = 8

cfg.SOLVER.BASE_LR = 0.001

cfg.SOLVER.MAX_ITER = 30000 # adjust based on your GPU

cfg.MODEL.ROI_HEADS.NUM_CLASSES = 7 # our 7 region types

os.makedirs(cfg.OUTPUT_DIR, exist_ok=True)

trainer = DefaultTrainer(cfg)

trainer.resume_or_load(resume=False)

trainer.train()

print("Training done! Model saved in", cfg.OUTPUT_DIR)

Running inference on documents

Module 7 — Custom Detection Heads for Document Layout

Goal: Learn how to take any detection model (YOLO, Detectron2, or our future DiT) and tweak its “final brain” so it stops thinking about cats and cars and starts understanding real document regions like titles and tables.

What detection heads are

The detection head is the last part of the model — the one that actually makes the decision. It takes rich features from the backbone (the part we trained in earlier modules) and outputs three things:

1. Bounding box coordinates

2. Confidence score

3. Class name (Title? Table? Paragraph?)

Think of it like the “judge” at the end of the pipeline. Without a good judge, even the best backbone gives useless results.

Why custom heads are required for documents

Default heads in YOLO or Detectron2 are trained on COCO dataset (80 everyday objects like person, car, dog).

But our documents have completely different “objects”:

- Titles are big and bold at the top

- Tables have straight lines and grids

- Paragraphs are long rectangular blocks

If you use the default head, the model will try to call your invoice title a “person” or your table a “bus”. Total confusion!

That’s why we must customize the head, change the number of classes and sometimes the output format to match document reality.

Module 8 — Evaluating Layout Detection Models

Goal: Learn how to check if your layout detector (YOLO, Detectron2, or later DiT) is actually good, not just “it looks okay” but real numbers that tell you it will work on messy Bhopal invoices and contracts.

Importance of model evaluation

Training is easy. Knowing if your model is actually useful? That’s the hard part. Without proper evaluation, you might think your model is 95% accurate… but when you put it on real scanned invoices from New Market shops, it fails on tables and costs the shopkeeper hours of manual fixing. Evaluation gives you honest scores so you can improve before deploying.

Intersection over Union (IoU)

This is the most important number in layout detection. IoU measures how much your predicted box overlaps with the true box.

Formula (super simple):

python

def compute_iou(pred_box, gt_box):

# pred_box and gt_box = [x1, y1, x2, y2]

x1 = max(pred_box[0], gt_box[0])

y1 = max(pred_box[1], gt_box[1])

x2 = min(pred_box[2], gt_box[2])

y2 = min(pred_box[3], gt_box[3])

inter_area = max(0, x2 - x1) * max(0, y2 - y1)

pred_area = (pred_box[2] - pred_box[0]) * (pred_box[3] - pred_box[1])

gt_area = (gt_box[2] - gt_box[0]) * (gt_box[3] - gt_box[1])

union_area = pred_area + gt_area - inter_area

return inter_area / union_area if union_area > 0 else 0.0

- IoU = 1.0 → perfect match

- IoU ≥ 0.5 → usually counted as correct

- IoU ≥ 0.9 → super accurate (what we want for tables!)

Real example: On a grocery invoice, if your predicted table box overlaps 92% with the real table, you get IoU=0.92 — excellent!

Precision and recall

-Precision→ Of all the regions your model predicted, how many were actually correct? (Avoids false alarms)

- Recall → Of all the real regions in the document, how many did your model find? (Avoids missing important stuff)

python

# Simple example on 10 predictions

true_positives = 8 # correctly found Title + Table etc.

false_positives = 2 # model predicted extra "Table"

false_negatives = 1 # missed one Footer

precision = true_positives / (true_positives + false_positives) # 8/10 = 0.80

recall = true_positives / (true_positives + false_negatives) # 8/9 = 0.89

F1 score combines both:

`f1 = 2 * precision * recall / (precision + recall)`

Mean Average Precision (mAP)

This is the king of all metrics in research papers. mAP averages precision across all classes (Title, Table, etc.) and across different IoU thresholds (0.5, 0.6…0.95).

Higher mAP = better model.

PubLayNet papers report mAP@0.5:0.95 — the toughest test.

Module 9 — Introduction to Multimodal Document Understanding

Goal: Understand why just detecting boxes (titles, tables, etc.) is not the end of the story and how the next level, multimodal models, finally makes Document AI truly smart.

Limitations of layout detection models

Layout detection (YOLO or Detectron2) is awesome at drawing boxes and saying “this is a Table” or “this is a Title”.

But that’s all it does.

It cannot answer:

- What is written inside the table?

- Is this invoice total correct?

- What is the relationship between the date in the header and the amount in the table?

Real world example:

Your model perfectly detects the table on a kirana shop invoice. Great! But it has no idea that “Rice 10kg ₹850” + “Oil 5L ₹420” should add up to ₹1270. It just sees boxes. The shopkeeper still has to check the math manually. Layout detection gives structure… but zero understanding.

Need for document understanding beyond detection

We need the AI to actually READ and REASON about the document, not just point at parts.

We want it to:

- Extract key-value pairs (Invoice No. → 12345)

- Understand reading order (header → table → total)

- Answer questions (“What is the GST amount?”)

- Summarize or classify the whole document

That’s where multimodal document understanding comes in, the next big jump after layout detection.

Multimodal learning in documents

“Multimodal” simply means the model learns from multiple “modes” at the same time:

- Visual (the image/scan)

- Text (the actual words from OCR)

- Layout (the positions and boxes we detected earlier)

Instead of three separate steps, the model looks at all three together and understands the document like a human.

Combining text + layout + visual information

Here’s how the magic happens:

1. Visual → The image itself (DiT backbone sees fonts, bold text, lines)

2. Text→ OCR or built-in text extractor gives the actual words

3. Layout→ Bounding boxes tell “this text belongs to the Table region”

The model fuses all three so it knows:

“This bold text at top-left is the Title AND it says ‘INVOICE’ AND it is above the table.”

Simple high-level example (pseudocode, we’ll code real versions later):

python

# Multimodal input example

image = load_scan("bhopal_invoice.jpg")

text_tokens = ocr_engine(image) # ["INVOICE", "Date:", "Rice", "850"...]

layout_boxes = layout_detector(image) # [{"bbox": [...], "label": "Table"}]

# Fuse everything

fused_features = multimodal_model(

image=image,

text=text_tokens,

layout=layout_boxes

)

answer = fused_features.answer_question("What is the total amount?")

print(answer) # → "₹1498.6"

Introduction to transformer-based document models

They are transformer-based (the same tech behind ChatGPT but for documents).

- They take image patches (like DiT)

- Add text embeddings

- Add layout (x,y) position embeddings

- Then the transformer “reads” everything together

Result? The model doesn’t just see pixels or words, it understands the whole document as one intelligent unit.

Real world example:

A bank statement comes in. Layout model finds the table. Multimodal model then reads the table + header date + footer bank name and automatically extracts every transaction with correct dates and amounts, no human needed.

• how multimodal models help understand documents (by combining visual + text + layout information)

Module 10 — LayoutLMv3 Architecture

Goal: Meet the first true multimodal superstar, LayoutLMv3 and see exactly how it combines text, layout, and vision to understand documents way better than detection alone.

Evolution of LayoutLM models

The LayoutLM family is like the iPhone series of Document AI, every new version gets way smarter.

- LayoutLMv1 (2020): Added bounding boxes to BERT so the model knew “where” the words were.

- LayoutLMv2 (2021): Started looking at the actual image too.

- LayoutLMv3 (2022): The current king. It’s simpler, faster, stronger, and treats text and image patches almost the same way (just like our DiT does for vision).

Microsoft open-sourced it, and today banks, law firms, and government offices in India use it daily.

Overview of LayoutLMv3

LayoutLMv3 is a multimodal transformer built specifically for documents. Instead of doing OCR → Layout Detection → Understanding in separate steps, it does everything together in one model. It can read, locate, and reason about the whole page at once.

It powers tasks like:

- Auto-extracting totals from invoices

- Filling forms

- Answering questions on scanned contracts

This is exactly the “beyond detection” multimodal magic we talked about in Module 9.

Inputs used in LayoutLMv3

LayoutLMv3 takes three things together (this is the secret sauce):

1. Text Tokens— The actual words (from OCR or tokenizer)

2.Bounding Boxes — The (x1, y1, x2, y2) position of every word or block (normalized 0–1000)

3. Image Features— Raw visual patches from the scanned document (exactly like DiT’s 16×16 patches)

Here’s how simple the input preparation looks in code:

Python

from transformers import LayoutLMv3Processor

from PIL import Image

processor = LayoutLMv3Processor.from_pretrained("microsoft/layoutlmv3-base")

image = Image.open("bhopal_invoice_scan.jpg").convert("RGB")

# From your layout detector + OCR

words = ["INVOICE", "#12345", "Rice", "10 kg", "₹850"]

boxes = [[120, 80, 380, 120], [450, 85, 720, 110], [50, 260, 150, 280], ...] # normalized

encoding = processor(

image=image,

text=words,

boxes=boxes,

return_tensors="pt",

max_length=512,

truncation=True

)

print("All three inputs ready → text + boxes + image patches!")

Multimodal transformer architecture

Everything goes into one big Transformer:

- Text tokens → word embeddings

- Bounding boxes → special 2D position embeddings (so the model knows “this word is inside the table”)

- Image patches → visual embeddings (same patch idea as DiT)

Then the transformer layers mix them all using attention. The model learns relationships like “the word ‘Total’ at the bottom of this table is the grand total”.

It also uses smart pre-training: Masked Language Modeling (hide words) + Masked Image Modeling (hide patches) + Word-Patch Alignment (match text to image regions).

How LayoutLM understands document structure

Because it sees text + layout + image together, LayoutLMv3 develops real intelligence.

Real-world example from Bhopal:

A grocery shop scans daily invoices. Plain OCR gives flat text. Layout detection gives boxes. But LayoutLMv3 sees:

- The word “Amount” (text)

- Sitting inside a table region at the bottom (layout box)

- Next to bold numbers with a line above (visual cue)

So it confidently extracts:

“Item: Rice | Qty: 10 kg | Amount: ₹850”

and even checks “Grand Total = ₹1498.6”.

No extra rules needed, the model just understands the whole document like a human accountant.

Reader understands:

• how LayoutLMv3 combines text, layout, and visual information in one powerful multimodal transformer

Module 11 — LayoutLMv3 with Layout Detection

Goal: Put everything together, use your layout detector (YOLO or Detectron2) first, then feed the clean regions into LayoutLMv3 so the model finally understands the full document.

Using layout detectors before LayoutLM

LayoutLMv3 is powerful, but it works best when you give it clean, meaningful regions instead of raw messy OCR output.

That’s why we always do this order:

1. Run layout detection (YOLO/Detectron2) → get big boxes for Title, Table, Paragraph, etc.

2. Run OCR only inside those boxes (much more accurate!)

3. Feed everything to LayoutLMv3

This way the model knows “these words belong to the Table region” before it even starts reading. Huge accuracy boost!

Real Bhopal example:

A bank in TT Nagar gets scanned statements. Without layout first, LayoutLMv3 gets confused by footer text. With layout detection first, it perfectly knows the transaction table region and extracts dates + amounts correctly every time.

Preparing inputs for LayoutLMv3

We need three things ready:

- The image

- Text words (from OCR)

- Bounding boxes (from layout detector, normalized 0–1000)

Here’s the exact preparation code:

python

import torch

{"label": "Table", "bbox": [50, 180, 720, 480]}, # normalized later

{"label": "Header", "bbox": [100, 50, 600, 120]},

# ... more regions

]

words = []

for region in layout_results:

# Crop or mask OCR to this region (simple version shown)

region_boxes = [[120, 200, 250, 220], [300, 200, 400, 220], ...] # actual coords

boxes.extend(region_boxes)

width, height = image.size

normalized_boxes = [[int(x1/width*1000), int(y1/height*1000),

print("Inputs ready: words + normalized boxes + image!")

Integrating OCR + layout detection

Now combine them into one clean pipeline:

python

def prepare_layoutlm_inputs(image_path, layout_model, ocr_reader):

image = Image.open(image_path).convert("RGB")

# 1. Get layout regions

layout_results = layout_model(image) # YOLO or Detectron2 output

# 2. Run OCR only on important regions

words = []

boxes = []

for region in layout_results: # only Table, Title, etc.

if region['label'] in ['Table', 'Title', 'Paragraph']:

# Crop or run OCR on region (EasyOCR example)

cropped = image.crop(region['bbox'])

ocr_result = ocr_reader.readtext(cropped)

for _, text, _ in ocr_result:

words.append(text)

boxes.append(region['bbox']) # same box for all words in region

# 3. Process for LayoutLMv3

processor = LayoutLMv3Processor.from_pretrained("microsoft/layoutlmv3-base")

encoding = processor(image, words, boxes=boxes,

return_tensors="pt", max_length=512, truncation=True)

return encoding

Feeding document regions into LayoutLMv3

Now the model gets the full picture:

from transformers import LayoutLMv3ForTokenClassification

model = LayoutLMv3ForTokenClassification.from_pretrained(

Example pipeline code

Here’s the complete end-to-end pipeline you can run today (copy-paste ready):

python

from PIL import Image

from ultralytics import YOLO

import easyocr

from transformers import LayoutLMv3ForTokenClassification, LayoutLMv3Processor

import torch

import json

# Load everything once

layout_model = YOLO('runs/detect/dit_layout_yolov8/weights/best.pt') # your trained YOLO

ocr_reader = easyocr.Reader(['en', 'hi'], gpu=False)

processor = LayoutLMv3Processor.from_pretrained("microsoft/layoutlmv3-base")

layoutlm_model = LayoutLMv3ForTokenClassification.from_pretrained(

"microsoft/layoutlmv3-base", num_labels=7

)

def full_document_pipeline(image_path):

image = Image.open(image_path).convert("RGB")

# Step 1: Layout Detection

layout_results = layout_model(image, conf=0.5)[0]

# Step 2: OCR inside important regions only

words = []

boxes = []

for box in layout_results.boxes:

cls = int(box.cls[0])

label = layout_model.names[cls]

if label in ["Table", "Title", "Paragraph"]:

# Simple crop + OCR (you can improve this)

x1, y1, x2, y2 = map(int, box.xyxy[0])

cropped = image.crop((x1, y1, x2, y2))

ocr_out = ocr_reader.readtext(cropped)

for _, text, _ in ocr_out:

words.append(text)

boxes.append([x1, y1, x2, y2]) # same box for words in region

# Step 3: Prepare for LayoutLMv3

width, height = image.size

norm_boxes = [[int(x1/width*1000), int(y1/height*1000),

int(x2/width*1000), int(y2/height*1000)] for x1,y1,x2,y2 in boxes]

encoding = processor(image, words, boxes=norm_boxes,

return_tensors="pt", max_length=512, truncation=True)

# Step 4: Run LayoutLMv3

with torch.no_grad():

outputs = layoutlm_model(**encoding)

# Step 5: Simple post-processing to structured data

predicted_labels = outputs.logits.argmax(-1)[0]

result = {"items": [], "grand_total": None}

for i, word in enumerate(words):

label = layoutlm_model.config.id2label[predicted_labels[i].item()]

if "amount" in label.lower():

try:

result["grand_total"] = float(word.replace("₹", "").replace(",", ""))

except:

pass

print(" Pipeline complete!")

return result

# RUN IT!

output = full_document_pipeline("bhopal_kirana_invoice.jpg")

print(json.dumps(output, indent=2))
